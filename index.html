Thai has 44 consonants divided into three classes that determine the tone of a syllable. High class (อักษรสูง) — 11 consonants. Mid class (อักษรกลาง) — 9 consonants. Low class (อักษรต่ำ) — 24 consonants. Consonants marked less common appear rarely in everyday Thai. Click any card to hear the example word. 🔊

Thai is a tonal language with 5 tones. The tone of a syllable is determined by: the consonant class (High / Mid / Low), the syllable type (live or dead), and the tone mark (if any). Mastering these 12 patterns lets you read any Thai syllable's tone.

The 12 Tone Patterns

Live syllable = ends in a long vowel or sonorant (ม น ง ว ล ร ย).   Dead syllable = ends in a stop (ก ด ต บ ป) or a short vowel with no final.

Note: Mid class dead syllables follow the same Low tone (เอก) as pattern 11. High class ๊ and ๋ marks are rarely used.

Special Rules

ห นำ — Leading ห

When ห precedes a Low Class sonorant (ม น ง ว ล ร ย ญ ณ), it is silent but converts the sonorant's tone class from Low → High, giving High Class tone patterns (patterns 6–9).

  • หมา hǎa — dog (ม is LC, but ห นำ → Rising tone like HC)
  • หนู hǔu — mouse (น is LC, ห นำ → Rising tone)
  • หนาว hǎao — cold (น is LC, ห นำ → Rising tone)

Live vs Dead Syllables — Quick Guide

  • Live syllable: ends in a long vowel, or a sonorant final (ม น ง ว ย). Tone follows the no-mark pattern.
  • Dead syllable — short: short vowel + stop final (ก ด ต บ ป), or bare short vowel. → High (LC) or Low (HC/MC).
  • Dead syllable — long: long vowel + stop final. → Falling (LC) or Low (HC/MC).
  • Final sonorants do NOT change the tone class — only the initial consonant's class matters.

Consonant Clusters

When two consonants begin a syllable (e.g. กล กร ปร), the first consonant (C1) determines the tone class. The second consonant (C2) is pronounced but does not affect tone.
